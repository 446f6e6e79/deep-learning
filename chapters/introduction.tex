\section{Introduction}
In this section, we will briefly introduce the basic concepts of deep learning and its historical background.

\subsection{Basic Definitions}
\paragraph{AI}
Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a discipline that has the ambition to create machines that
mimic human intelligence. It encompasses a wide range of techniques and approaches, including rule-based systems, expert systems, and machine learning.
The ultimate goal of AI is to develop systems capable of learning from experience as effectively as humans do.

\paragraph{Machine Learning}
Machine Learning (ML) is a subset of AI that enables machines to learn
from experience (i.e., data) to improve their performance on a specific task.

\paragraph{Deep Learning}
This is a subfield of Machine Learning that particularly focuses on
\textbf{Artificial Neural Networks} (ANNs). The term `deep' refers to the use of multiple layers
in the network, which allows it to learn complex representations of data, with multiple levels of abstraction.

\subsection{Historical Background}
The concept of artificial neural networks dates back to the 1940s, with the work of Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts, who proposed a simple model that
attempted to emulate the behavior of biological neurons.
This is regarded as the first single-layer perceptron model, which is a fundamental building block of deep learning.

\paragraph{Electronic Brain}
In the first perceptron model, the inputs form a vector of \textbf{binary features} that are simply summed together and passed through a \textbf{threshold function},
without any \textbf{weights} involved.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \scalebox{0.8}{
        \input{Tikz/introduction/electronic_brain.tex}
    }
    \caption{Architecture of the electronic brain model. Inputs $x_i$ are summed together and passed through an activation function $\sigma$ to produce the output $\hat{y}$.}
    \label{fig:electronic_brain}
\end{figure}
It can be shown that this model is capable of classifying linearly separable data, such as the AND, OR, and NOT functions, but it fails to classify non-linearly separable data, such as the XOR function.

\paragraph{The Perceptron}
In the 1950s, Frank Rosenblatt introduced the \textbf{Perceptron}, which is an extension of the electronic brain model.
The key difference is the introduction of \textbf{weights} for each input, which allows the model to learn from data and adjust its parameters accordingly.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \scalebox{0.8}{
        \input{Tikz/introduction/perceptron.tex}
    }
    \caption{Architecture of a single layer perceptron. Inputs $x_i$ are weighted by $w_i$, summed together, and passed through an activation function $\sigma$ to produce the output $y$.}
    \label{fig:single_perceptron}
\end{figure}
The prediction $\hat{y}$ of the perceptron is given by the following formula:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{y} = \sigma\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} \theta_i x_i + \theta_0\right)
\end{equation}
where $\theta_i$ are the weights associated with each input $x_i$, and $\sigma$ is the activation function.
We can also express the prediction in vector form as follows:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{y} = \sigma\left(\boldsymbol{\theta}^T \boldsymbol{x} + \theta_0\right)
\end{equation}

The most important intuition is that the weights $\theta_i$ are not fixed, but rather learned from data. 

The bias term is introduced to allow the model to shift the decision boundary (hyperplane) away from the origin.

\paragraph{Perceptron Learning Algorithm}
\label{perceptron-training}
The weights of the perceptron are learned from data using the \textbf{Perceptron Training Rule}.
Given a set of training examples $\mathcal{T} = \{(\boldsymbol{x_1}, y_1), (\boldsymbol{x_2}, y_2), \ldots, (\boldsymbol{x_N}, y_N)\}$, $y_i \in \{0, 1\}$
and a learning rate $\eta$, the weights are updated as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Initialize the weights $\theta_i$ to small random values;
    \item Until convergence, repeat:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Select randomly a training example $(\boldsymbol{x_i}, y_i)$ from $\mathcal{T}$;
        \item Compute the prediction $\hat{y}_i$ using the current weights:
        \[ \hat{y}_i = \sigma\left(\boldsymbol{\theta}^T \boldsymbol{x_i} + \theta_0 \right) \]
        \item Update the weights based on the error between the prediction and the true label:
        \[ \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t+1)} \leftarrow \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)} + \eta (y_i - \hat{y}_i) \boldsymbol{x_i} \]
        \item Update the bias:
        \[ \theta_0^{(t+1)} \leftarrow \theta_0^{(t)} + \eta (y_i - \hat{y}_i) \]
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}
Note that we are assuming that the training data is linearly separable, otherwise we would have to introduce a stopping criterion to prevent the algorithm from running indefinitely.

\paragraph{Perceptron Today}
Today, the activation function $\sigma$ is typically a non-linear function, such as the sigmoid, ReLU, or tanh function, 
which allows the model to learn complex patterns in the data.
\definition{Perceptron}{
    An artificial neuron, i.e., a perceptron is a non-linear parametrized function
    with a restricted output range.
}

\paragraph{Perceptron Problem}
As anticipated, in the 1970s it was found that the perceptron was not able to learn non-linearly separable data, such as the XOR function.
This was seen as a major setback for the field of AI, and it led to a decline in interest and funding for research in this area, which is now known as the "AI winter".

\paragraph{MLP}
AI did not stop there, and in the 1980s, researchers introduced the concept of \textbf{Multilayer Perceptron} (MLP). This architecture
had a major issue, which was the difficulty in computing the weights of the hidden layers.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \scalebox{0.8}{
        \input{Tikz/introduction/mlp.tex}
    }
    \caption{Architecture of a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) with three hidden layers, fully connected in a feed-forward fashion.}
    \label{fig:mlp}
\end{figure}
This structure was very flexible as multiple nodes could be used in the output layer, so it could be used for multi-class classification problems, and also for regression.

\paragraph{Backpropagation}
\label{introduction-backpropagation}
The perceptron learning algorithm was not suitable for training MLPs, as no expected output was available for the hidden layers, but just for the output layer.

Backpropagation is an efficient algorithm for computing the gradients also for large datasets.
At a high level, the algorithm can be summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Forward propagation}: Given an input $\boldsymbol{x}$, compute the output of the network $\hat{y}$ by 
    propagating the input through the layers of the network;
    \item \textbf{Loss computation}: Compute the loss $L(y, f(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta}))$, where $y$ is the true label and $f(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta})$ is the output of the network with parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$;
    \item \textbf{Backward propagation}: the error signal is propagated backwards through the network and it's used to update the weights.
\end{enumerate}
Obviosly, this concept will be covered in more detail in the next chapters.

\paragraph{Other Architectures}
Other architectures were proposed in the following years that lead to the current breakthrough of deep learning, such as:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Convolutional Neural Networks} (CNNs), which are particularly effective for image processing tasks;
    \item \textbf{Recurrent Neural Networks} (RNNs), used for sequential data, such as time series and natural language processing;
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Cybenko's Theorem}
Cybenko's theorem states that a 2-layer feed-forward neural network with linear output
can approximate any continuous function on a compact subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ with arbitrary accuracy.

In practice, the more hidden layers we have, the more complex functions we can learn. Deep
neural networks need a lot of labeled data to be trained, and cannot be trained easily.

\paragraph{Second Winter of AI}
Three main factors contributed to the second AI winter in the late 1980s:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Deep Neural Networks} tend to overfit the training data, and had problems
    with vanishing gradients, which made them difficult to train;
    \item \textbf{Lack of computational resources} to train deep neural networks. In fact, no GPUs were available at the time;
    \item \textbf{Lack of large datasets} to train deep neural networks.
\end{itemize}
All these issues led to a decline in interest and funding for research in this area, with an increase
of interest in other areas of ML, such as Support Vector Machines (SVMs) and Kernel Methods.

\paragraph{First Deep Learning Applications - AlexNet}
In 2012, a deep convolutional neural network called AlexNet won the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC) with a top-5 error rate of 15.3\%, 
which was significantly better than the second-best entry, which had an error rate of 26.2\%.
This was a major breakthrough for the field of deep learning, and it led to a shift of interest and funding back to this area, which is now known as the "Deep Learning Revolution".

AlexNet also shifted the focus of research from shallow architectures to deep architectures. The first one
required heavy works on feature engineering before training the model, while the second one was able to learn features directly from the data, without any human intervention.
